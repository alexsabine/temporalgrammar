Abstract

 

Before delving into the mathematical machinery of Coherence (C) - Rupture (δ) - Regeneration (R), we establish its philosophical foundation. CRR offers a possible mathematical extension of non-Markovian dynamics, a formal language for describing how systems maintain identity through discontinuous change. At its core lies a tripartite temporal ontology that maps cleanly onto human phenomenological experience:

 

  • Past $\rightarrow$ Coherence C: The accumulated weight of all that has been
  • Present $\rightarrow$ Rupture $\delta$: The instantaneous now where decisions crystalise
  • Future $\rightarrow$ Regeneration $\mathcal{R}_{regen}$: The exponentially-weighted projection of history forward

    •  

    This CRR conjecture is that these mathematical structures are not arbitrary, but philosophically necessary for describing agents—physical, biological, or cognitive—that exist through time rather than merely in time.

     

     

    1. The Phenomenology of Temporal Experience

     

    1.1 Three Irreducible Aspects of Time

     

    Human experience suggests three irreducible aspects of temporality:

     

    The Past as Accumulation

    CRR proposes that we do not experience the past as a collection of discrete, independent moments. Rather, past experiences accumulate into a continuous field of memory. Each moment adds to the total; nothing is truly discarded. A 40-year-old carries all 40 years, not just the present instant. This is non-Markovian temporality: the present state depends on the entire history, not just the immediately prior state.

     

    The Present as Metabolic Decision Point

    Yet we also experience a radical nowness—moments when accumulated history crystalises into action. These are not continuous transitions but discrete punctuations: the decision to speak, the snap of a synapse firing, the moment of understanding. In these instants, the system "metabolises" its past and commits to a new trajectory. This is instantaneous rupture: the delta function $\delta$(t-t₀) that localises time to an instantaneous point, discretized when in physical form (e.g. a brain)

     

    The Future as Weighted Projection

    Finally, we experience the future not as blank but as conditioned by accumulated history. A person with deep training responds differently than a novice, not because of the immediate past, but because of the exponentially weighted integral of all past practice. In CRR, history doesn't influence the future uniformly; recent, high-coherence moments matter more. This is regeneration: the exp(C/$\Omega$)-weighted projection forward.

     

    1.2 The Problem: Standard Physics often misses This Structure

     

    Classical mechanics is instantaneously Markovian: F = ma depends only on current state (x, ẋ). The past is irrelevant.

     

    x_{t-1}, x_{t-2}, ...) = P(x_t

     

    Quantum mechanics has entanglement and memory through the Nakajima-Zwanzig formalism, but lacks:

  • Discrete ruptures (except measurement, which is ad hoc)
  • Exponentially weighted regeneration (memory kernels decay, not amplify)

    •  

    The gap: Standard physics cannot describe systems that:

  • Accumulate memory continuously (Coherence)
  • Undergo instantaneous reconfigurations (Rupture)
  • Rebuild themselves using exponentially-weighted history (Regeneration)

    •  

    Yet such systems pervade nature: brains, immune systems, ecosystems, even black holes undergoing phase transitions.

C[x](t) = ∫₀^t L(x(τ), ẋ(τ), τ) dτ

     

    Mathematical Structure:

  • Integral over entire past: $\tau$ $\in$ [0, t], not just recent history
  • Path-dependent: C depends on the trajectory x(·), not just current x(t)
  • Extensive quantity: [C] = Energy, like work or entropy
  • Non-decreasing (if L $\geq$ 0): Time's arrow is built in

    •  

    Phenomenological Interpretation:

  • L(x,ẋ,$\tau$) is the rate at which experience accumulates at moment $\tau$
  • High ẋ or far-from-equilibrium x $\rightarrow$ higher L $\rightarrow$ more coherence built
  • C is the system's memory burden—the total weight of history it carries

  • Immune system: C = affinity maturation progress
  • Black hole: C = infalling matter's integrated energy
  • Consciousness: C = accumulated experience/learning

A musician practices for years; the performance draws disproportionately on recent intense rehearsal (high C) rather than distant early lessons. This is exponential weighting.

  • When C($\tau$) > $\Omega$: Weight >> 1 (critical states dominate)

  • Timescale $\tau$_char: Sets regeneration rate

  • Slow L $\rightarrow$ long $\tau$_char $\rightarrow$ slow reconstruction

  • [$\phi$] = velocity: It's not passive memory but active tendency
  • $\phi$ could be mean past state, attractor, or goal state

  • The future is not determined solely by the present
  • Nor is it determined by a linear average of all past states
  • Instead: exponentially-weighted history shapes the future
  • States with high coherence C($\tau$) exert disproportionate influence
  • This is why intensity matters as much as duration

  • Trauma response: High-C events (intense) have lasting, disproportionate impact

    Philosophical Status: Regeneration is the formal structure of habituation modified by intensity. Hume said habit comes from repetition; CRR adds: habit comes from exponentially-weighted repetition. One transformative moment (C $\rightarrow$ $\Omega$) outweighs years of routine.

    Core Mathematical Principles

    The Canonical CRR Equation

    The Coherence-Rupture-Regeneration (CRR) framework synthesises three established mathematical structures into a single dynamical equation that describes how systems accumulate memory, undergo discontinuous transitions, and regenerate through weighted historical integration.

    $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x) + \int_0^t \mathcal{K}(t-\tau) x(\tau) d\tau + \int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) \cdot e^{C(\tau)/\Omega} \Theta(t-\tau) d\tau + \sum_i \rho_i \delta(t-t_i)$$ $$C(x,t) = \int_0^t L(x,\tau) d\tau$$
    Component Breakdown:
    • $f(x)$: Smooth evolution (standard dynamical systems)
    • $\int_0^t \mathcal{K}(t-\tau) x(\tau) d\tau$: Memory kernel (Nakajima-Zwanzig)
    • $\int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) \cdot e^{C(\tau)/\Omega} \Theta(t-\tau) d\tau$: Regeneration operator (novel contribution)
    • $\sum_i \rho_i \delta(t-t_i)$: Rupture events (Lévy processes)
    • $C(x,t)$: Accumulated coherence functional

    Foundation 1: Variational Mechanics

    CRR begins from Hamilton's principle of stationary action, which states that physical systems evolve along paths that extremise the action functional:

    $$\delta S = \delta \int_0^T \mathcal{L}(x, \dot{x}, t) dt = 0$$

    This yields the Euler-Lagrange equations:

    $$\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = 0$$

    where $\mathcal{L}$ is the Lagrangian (distinct from the memory density $L$). CRR extends this to incorporate non-Markovian memory and discrete transitions.

    Foundation 2: Nakajima-Zwanzig Memory Kernel

    For open systems coupled to environments, the Nakajima-Zwanzig projection operator method yields dynamics with memory:

    $$\frac{d\rho}{dt} = \mathcal{P}\mathcal{L}\rho(t) + \int_0^t \mathcal{K}(t-\tau) \rho(\tau) d\tau$$ Where: $\mathcal{P}\mathcal{L}$ = Projected evolution operator $\mathcal{K}(t-\tau)$ = Memory kernel (environment back-action)
    Physical Meaning: The memory kernel $\mathcal{K}$ encodes how the environment's past states continue to influence the system's present dynamics. This creates non-Markovian evolution where the future depends on the entire history, not just the current state.

    Memory Density and Coherence Accumulation

    In CRR, we define a memory density field $L(x,\tau)$ that captures the rate at which coherence accumulates:

    $$L(x,\tau) = \text{memory density (can be positive or negative)}$$ $$C(x,t) = \int_0^t L(x,\tau) d\tau \text{ (coherence functional)}$$

    Key properties:

    • $L > 0$: Coherence building (memory integration, learning)
    • $L < 0$: Decoherence (forgetting, dissipation)
    • $C(t)$: Total accumulated coherence (can be positive or negative)

    Foundation 3: Lévy Jump-Diffusion Processes

    Systems exhibiting sudden state changes follow jump-diffusion dynamics:

    $$dx = f(x)dt + \sigma(x)dW + \sum_i \rho_i \delta(t-t_i)$$ Components: $f(x)dt$ = Drift term (deterministic evolution) $\sigma(x)dW$ = Diffusion (Brownian noise) $\rho_i \delta(t-t_i)$ = Discrete jumps at rupture times {$t_i$}

    The Dirac delta function $\delta(t-t_i)$ represents an instantaneous discontinuous transition, creating punctuated equilibrium dynamics.

    Rupture Triggering Conditions

    In CRR, ruptures occur when coherence reaches a critical threshold:

    Rupture occurs when: $C(x,t) \geq C_{\text{crit}}$ Or when: $\left|\frac{dC}{dt}\right| > \theta_{\text{critical}}$ Where $\Omega$ is the system temperature parameter

    Novel CRR Contribution: Exponentially-Weighted Regeneration

    The genuinely new element of CRR is the regeneration operator:

    $$R[\chi](x,t) = \int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) \cdot e^{C(\tau)/\Omega} \cdot \Theta(t-\tau) d\tau$$ Where: $\phi(x,\tau)$ = Historical field signal (past states) $e^{C(\tau)/\Omega}$ = Exponential memory weighting $\Theta(t-\tau)$ = Heaviside function (causality constraint) $\Omega$ = Normalization temperature parameter
    Why Exponential Weighting? The $e^{C/\Omega}$ factor means that regeneration is weighted by accumulated coherence. States with high coherence have exponentially stronger influence on future dynamics. This creates a Boltzmann-like distribution over historical states, similar to thermodynamic ensembles.

    Causality and the Heaviside Function

    The Heaviside step function $\Theta(t-\tau)$ ensures causality:

    $$\Theta(t-\tau) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \tau \leq t \text{ (past influences present)} \\ 0 & \text{if } \tau > t \text{ (future cannot influence past)} \end{cases}$$

    This enforces the "arrow of time" - only past states with $\tau \leq t$ contribute to the regeneration integral. This temporal asymmetry distinguishes CRR from time-symmetric physics. Note that the Heaviside is "belt and braces" for specific applications of CRR in some systems.

    Thermodynamic Consistency

    Between rupture events, CRR dynamics satisfy the Second Law of Thermodynamics. We define a free energy functional:

    $$F(x,t) = E(x,t) - \Omega \log(1 + C(x,t))$$ Theorem: $\frac{dF}{dt} \leq 0$ (entropy production $\geq 0$) Proof: $\frac{dF}{dt} = \frac{dE}{dt} - \frac{\Omega L(x,t)}{1 + C(x,t)}$ For dissipative dynamics ($\frac{dE}{dt} \leq 0$), this yields $\frac{dF}{dt} \leq 0$. ∎

    This ensures CRR is thermodynamically consistent with established physics.

    Markovian vs Non-Markovian Regimes

    Regime Condition Behavior
    Pure Markovian $C \approx 0$ or $\Omega \to \infty$ Memory integral vanishes; future depends only on present state
    Non-Markovian $C > 0$ and finite $\Omega$ Past history exponentially weighted; system "remembers"
    Critical Transition $C \to C_{\text{crit}}$ Rupture occurs; system resets to near-Markovian state

    Coherence as Integrated Free Energy Reduction

    The fundamental link between coherence and free energy can be derived rigorously:

    Free energy decomposition in FEP: $$F = \text{Complexity} - \text{Accuracy}$$ $$F = D_{KL}[q(s|o)||p(s)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(o|s)]$$ When learning proceeds successfully: 1. Complexity term decreases (q → p) 2. Accuracy term increases (better predictions) 3. Net result: F decreases Coherence as integrated surprise reduction: $$C(t) = \int_0^t [\text{Accuracy}(\tau) - \text{Complexity}(\tau)] d\tau = -\int_0^t F(\tau) d\tau$$
    Coherence is negative integrated free energy. High coherence means the system has successfully minimised surprise over time.

    Ruptures occur when the current generative model encounters observations it cannot assimilate:

    Rupture condition: $$\delta(t-t_0) \text{ fires when:}$$ 1. $C(t) \geq C_{\text{crit}} - \epsilon$ (coherence saturation) AND 2. $\exists o_{\text{new}}: F(o_{\text{new}}|\text{model}_{\text{current}}) > \theta_{\text{critical}}$ where $\theta_{\text{critical}} \approx 3\sigma_F$ (three standard deviations above mean F)

    Catastrophe Theory and Phase Transitions

    CRR as a Theory of Critical Transitions

    Catastrophe theory, developed by René Thom, studies how systems undergo sudden qualitative changes when control parameters vary smoothly. CRR provides a mathematical framework for such transitions in systems with memory.

    Classical Catastrophe Theory

    In catastrophe theory, systems evolve on a potential surface $V(x, c)$ where $x$ is the state and $c$ are control parameters:

    System state minimizes potential: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} = 0$$ Catastrophe occurs when: $$\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2} = 0 \text{ (stability loss)}$$ Example - Cusp catastrophe: $$V(x, a, b) = \frac{x^4}{4} + \frac{a x^2}{2} + bx$$

    As control parameters $a$ and $b$ vary, the system can suddenly jump between stable states - a catastrophic transition.

    CRR Extension: Memory-Dependent Catastrophes

    CRR extends classical catastrophe theory by making the potential landscape itself dependent on accumulated history:

    Memory-modified potential: $$V(x, c, C) = V_0(x, c) + V_{\text{memory}}(x, C)$$ Where: $$V_{\text{memory}}(x, C) = -\Omega \log(1 + C(x,t))$$ Critical condition becomes: $$C(x,t) \geq C_{\text{crit}} \Rightarrow \text{rupture}$$
    Novel Feature: Unlike classical catastrophe theory where control parameters change externally, in CRR the system's own history (coherence accumulation) drives it toward critical points. This is self-organised criticality.

    The Seven Elementary Catastrophes and CRR

    Thom identified seven elementary catastrophes. CRR provides a unifying framework:

    Catastrophe Type Classical Signature CRR Interpretation
    Fold Simple jump between states Basic rupture: $C \to C_{\text{crit}}$ → jump to lower C
    Cusp Hysteresis and bistability Fragile signature: different rupture thresholds for forward/reverse
    Swallowtail Three-state system Dialectical signature: synthesis from competing coherence fields
    Butterfly Four-state system Complex adaptive signatures with multiple rupture pathways

    Self-Organized Criticality in CRR

    SOC condition in CRR: 1. Coherence accumulates: $C(t) = \int_0^t L(\tau) d\tau$ with $\langle L \rangle > 0$ 2. System approaches criticality: $C(t) \to C_{\text{crit}}$ 3. Rupture occurs: $\delta(t-t_0)$ at $C = C_{\text{crit}}$ 4. Coherence resets: $C \to pC$ (partial preservation) 5. Cycle repeats: System re-accumulates toward next rupture Result: Power-law distribution of rupture sizes (avalanches)

    Avalanche Statistics

    CRR predicts that rupture amplitudes $\rho_i$ follow power-law distributions:

    $$P(\rho) \sim \rho^{-\alpha}$$ where $\alpha$ depends on the preservation parameter $p$: $$\alpha = \frac{2}{1-p}$$ Examples: - $p = 0.5$ (resilient): $\alpha = 4$ (rapid decay) - $p = 0.0$ (chaotic): $\alpha = 2$ (slow decay) - $p \to 1$ (fragile): $\alpha \to \infty$ (catastrophic jumps)

    Critical $\Omega \sim C_{\text{crit}}$ Balanced coherence-rupture dynamics Resilient or oscillatory
    Frozen $\Omega \to \infty$ Memory weighting weak; catastrophic transitions Fragile signature

Fixed point analysis (between ruptures): $$C^* = L_0/\lambda$$ Stability: $$\frac{d}{dC}\left(\frac{dC}{dt}\right) = -\lambda < 0$$ (stable) Rupture threshold creates effective bifurcation at $C = C_{\text{crit}}$

    Hysteresis and Memory Effects

    CRR naturally produces hysteresis - path-dependent behavior where the system's response depends on its history:

    Forward path (coherence building): $$C_{\text{rupture, forward}} = C_{\text{crit}}$$ Backward path (after rupture): $$C_{\text{rupture, backward}} = pC_{\text{crit}} < C_{\text{crit}}$$ Hysteresis width: $$\Delta C = C_{\text{crit}}(1-p)$$

    This creates a memory of the transition - the system "remembers" that it has already ruptured, making subsequent ruptures easier (if $p < 1$) or harder (if additional coherence must build).

    Catastrophic Forgetting and Machine Learning

    The Catastrophic Forgetting Problem

    Catastrophic forgetting (also called catastrophic interference) is one of the central problems in continual learning. When neural networks learn new tasks, they often completely overwrite what they learned on previous tasks.

    Standard Neural Networks as Fragile Signature Systems

    Current ML systems exhibit textbook fragile dynamics:

    • Monotonic coherence buildup: Weights accumulate information about current tasks with no mechanism for controlled release
    • No intermediate rupture: Networks have no way to selectively "forget" or reorganize before catastrophic thresholds
    • Catastrophic collapse: New tasks completely overwrite previous learning rather than integrating with it

    CRR-Based Resilient Learning Architecture

    A CRR approach would implement metabolized forgetting rather than trying to prevent forgetting entirely:

    Coherence monitoring: $$L(\theta, \dot{\theta}, t) = \text{integration quality}(\theta) - \text{interference cost}(\dot{\theta})$$ $$C(\theta, t) = \int_0^t L(\theta, \tau) d\tau$$ Controlled rupture: When coherence gradients indicate potential catastrophic interference, trigger selective forgetting: $$\rho_i(\theta) \cdot \delta(t-t_i) = \text{targeted weight reset}(\text{critical neurons}, \text{consolidation strength})$$ Memory-weighted regeneration: $$R[\theta](t) = \int_0^t \nabla_{\text{past}}(\tau) \cdot e^{C(\tau)/\Omega} d\tau$$

    Implementation Strategies

    1. Selective Consolidation

    Instead of preserving all weights equally, the memory integral preferentially protects connections that contributed to coherent past performance:

    Weight importance: $$\mathcal{I}_i = \sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{tasks}}} e^{C_j/\Omega} \cdot \left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_j}{\partial w_i}\right|^2$$ Protection during new task: $$w_i^{\text{new}} = w_i^{\text{old}} - \eta \left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{new}}}{\partial w_i} + \lambda \mathcal{I}_i (w_i - w_i^{\text{old}})\right)$$

    2. Anticipatory Rupture

    Monitor coherence gradients to trigger controlled forgetting before catastrophic interference, not after:

    Detect impending interference: $$\text{if } \left|\nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{new}}\right| \cdot \left|\nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\text{old}}\right| < -\theta \text{ then trigger rupture}$$ Selective reset: Reset weights with lowest $\mathcal{I}_i$ scores while preserving high-coherence connections

    3. Regenerative Plasticity

    New learning guided by weighted memory of past learning patterns, allowing genuine synthesis:

    Historical gradient field: $$\phi(\theta, \tau) = \nabla_{\theta} \mathcal{L}(\tau)$$ Regeneration-guided update: $$\Delta w = -\eta \nabla \mathcal{L}_{\text{new}} + \beta \int_0^t \phi(\theta, \tau) \cdot e^{C(\tau)/\Omega} d\tau$$ Where $\beta$ controls balance between new learning and historical guidance

    What is a Memory Signature?

    A memory signature is the characteristic pattern of how a system metabolizes coherence (C), ruptures (δ), and regenerates (R). It's not about what a system remembers, but how it remembers, ruptures, and regenerates.

    The Five Primary Signature Types

    1. Fragile Signature

    Mathematical Form: $L > 0$ accumulates monotonically, rupture thresholds too high (rare $t_i$)

    Dynamics: Long smooth coherence buildup → catastrophic rupture with massive loss

    • Neural: Epileptic seizure after runaway synchrony
    • Cultural: Rigid regimes that shatter under revolution
    • Personal: Repression, perfectionism → breakdown

    2. Resilient Signature

    Mathematical Form: Moderate $L$, rupture at intermediate thresholds, efficient regeneration

    Dynamics: Coherence builds → rupture before catastrophic levels → regeneration recycles memory → identity preserved

    Examples:

    • Ecological: Fire-adapted savannas that regrow stronger
    • Neural: Flexible attention switching without breakdown
    • Cultural: Democracies absorbing crises through reform
    • Personal: Therapy, reflection, adaptive coping

    Diagnostics: Coherence oscillates around stable mid-levels

    3. Oscillatory Signature

    Mathematical Form: $L$ alternates sign (positive ↔ negative); rupture times periodic

    4. Chaotic Signature

    Mathematical Form: Rupture thresholds extremely low, frequent impulses ($t_i$ dense, $\rho_i$ small)

    Examples:

    • Ecological: Degraded landscapes with constant small shocks

    • Personal: Chronic instability, inability to commit

    Diagnostics: Coherence distribution centered near zero with high-frequency noise

    Examples:

    • Ecological: Mixed-species forests stabilizing against disturbance

    • Personal: Integrating contradictions into wisdom
