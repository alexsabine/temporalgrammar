Coherence–Rupture–Regeneration

Mathematical Foundations for Non-Markovian Dynamics with Threshold-Triggered Discontinuities

A. Sabine · Cohere Research · Working Document v3.0

Mathematical Foundations

1. Motivation

Many natural systems exhibit dynamics characterised by three features: (i) gradual accumulation of some quantity over time, (ii) sudden discontinuous transitions when thresholds are reached, and (iii) reconstruction of state that depends on history in a non-uniform way. Examples include neural spiking, earthquake dynamics, immune responses, and developmental transitions.

The Coherence–Rupture–Regeneration (CRR) framework provides mathematical structures to describe such systems. This document presents the core definitions, derives key properties, and states conjectures that generate testable predictions.

2. Primitive Notions

We work with the following primitives:

  • A state space $\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$
  • A time parameter $t \in [0, T]$ for some $T > 0$ (or $T = \infty$)
  • A trajectory $x: [0,T] \to \mathcal{X}$

3. Core Definitions

Definition 1 (Memory Density)

The memory density is a function $L: \mathcal{X} \times [0,T] \to \mathbb{R}$ that assigns to each state-time pair $(x, \tau)$ a rate of coherence accumulation. We assume $L$ is measurable and locally integrable.

Definition 2 (Coherence)

The coherence at state $x$ and time $t$ is defined as:

$$C(x,t) := \int_0^t L(x,\tau) \, d\tau$$

When the trajectory $x(\cdot)$ is understood, we write $C(t) = \int_0^t L(x(\tau), \tau) \, d\tau$.

Remark

$C(x,t)$ measures accumulated "history" up to time $t$. Unlike Markovian systems where only the current state matters, CRR systems carry their past in $C$. Note that $L$ may be negative (decoherence), so $C$ need not be monotonic.

Definition 3 (Threshold Parameter)

The threshold parameter $\Omega > 0$ is a positive constant that characterises the system's capacity for coherence accumulation before structural change becomes necessary.

Definition 4 (Rupture)

A rupture occurs at time $t_i$ when $C(t_i^-) \geq \Omega$. The rupture is represented by a Dirac delta contribution $\rho_i \, \delta(t - t_i)$ to the system dynamics, where $\rho_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the rupture magnitude.

Following rupture, coherence resets according to:

$$C(t_i^+) = p \cdot C(t_i^-)$$

where $p \in [0,1)$ is the preservation parameter.

Definition 5 (Regeneration)

The regeneration operator is defined as:

$$R(x,t) := \int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) \cdot e^{C(x,\tau)/\Omega} \, d\tau$$

where $\phi: \mathcal{X} \times [0,T] \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is the historical field, specifying what content from each past moment contributes to reconstruction.

Remark

The exponential weighting $e^{C/\Omega}$ ensures that high-coherence historical states contribute disproportionately to regeneration. When $C(\tau) \ll \Omega$, the weight is approximately 1. When $C(\tau) \approx \Omega$, the weight is approximately $e \approx 2.718$. This creates a "soft attention" mechanism favouring significant historical moments.

4. The CRR Dynamical Equation

Definition 6 (CRR Dynamics)

A system follows CRR dynamics if its evolution satisfies:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x) + \int_0^t \mathcal{K}(t-\tau) \, x(\tau) \, d\tau + R(x,t) + \sum_{i: t_i \leq t} \rho_i \, \delta(t - t_i)$$

where:

  • $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a smooth vector field (local dynamics)
  • $\mathcal{K}: [0,T] \to \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is a memory kernel
  • $R(x,t)$ is the regeneration operator (Definition 5)
  • $\{t_i\}$ are rupture times satisfying Definition 4

5. Well-Posedness

Proposition 1 (Existence between ruptures)

Assume $f$ is locally Lipschitz, $\mathcal{K}$ is $L^1$, and $\phi$ is bounded. Then between any two consecutive rupture times $t_i$ and $t_{i+1}$, there exists a unique solution $x(t)$ to the CRR equation.

Between ruptures, the equation reduces to a Volterra integro-differential equation. Standard fixed-point arguments (cf. Gripenberg et al., 1990) yield local existence and uniqueness under the stated conditions.

Remark

Global existence requires additional conditions to ensure rupture times do not accumulate (Zeno behaviour). A sufficient condition is that $L$ is bounded above, ensuring $C$ grows at most linearly, so inter-rupture intervals have positive minimum length.

6. Key Properties

Proposition 2 (Non-Markovian character)

CRR dynamics are non-Markovian: the future evolution $x(t)$ for $t > s$ depends on the entire trajectory $\{x(\tau)\}_{\tau \in [0,s]}$, not merely on $x(s)$.

The regeneration term $R(x,t)$ explicitly integrates over $[0,t]$, making the evolution dependent on the full history.

Proposition 3 (Coherence bounds memory influence)

For the regeneration integral, the contribution from time $\tau$ is weighted by $e^{C(x,\tau)/\Omega}$. Thus:

  • If $\Omega \to \infty$: all weights $\to 1$ (uniform history)
  • If $\Omega \to 0^+$: weights concentrate on $\arg\max_\tau C(x,\tau)$ (dominated by peak coherence)

For large $\Omega$, $C/\Omega \to 0$, so $e^{C/\Omega} \to 1$. For small $\Omega$, the exponential amplifies differences in $C$, creating a peaked distribution.

Core Framework

1. The Rupture Mechanism

The rupture condition $C(t) \geq \Omega$ is central to CRR. We develop its mathematical consequences.

Proposition 4 (Inter-rupture dynamics)

Let $L(x,t) = \ell > 0$ be constant. Then ruptures occur at times $t_k = k \cdot \Delta t$ where:

$$\Delta t = \frac{\Omega(1-p)}{\ell}$$

assuming $C(0) = 0$ and perfect periodicity.

Between ruptures, $\frac{dC}{dt} = \ell$, so $C(t) = C(t_k^+) + \ell(t - t_k)$. The next rupture occurs when $C = \Omega$, i.e., when $p\Omega + \ell \Delta t = \Omega$, giving $\Delta t = \Omega(1-p)/\ell$.

Remark

In real systems, $L$ varies with state and time, introducing variability in inter-rupture intervals. This variability is characterised by the coefficient of variation $CV = \sigma_{\Delta t}/\mu_{\Delta t}$, which becomes a key observable.

2. The Regeneration Mechanism

Proposition 5 (Normalised regeneration)

Define the normalised regeneration weight:

$$w(\tau; t) := \frac{e^{C(\tau)/\Omega}}{\int_0^t e^{C(s)/\Omega} \, ds}$$

Then $w(\tau; t)$ defines a probability density on $[0,t]$, and the regeneration integral can be written:

$$R(x,t) = Z(t) \cdot \mathbb{E}_w[\phi]$$

where $Z(t) = \int_0^t e^{C(s)/\Omega} ds$ is the partition function and $\mathbb{E}_w[\phi]$ is the expectation of $\phi$ under $w$.

By construction, $\int_0^t w(\tau; t) d\tau = 1$ and $w \geq 0$. The rewriting follows from the definition of expectation.

Remark

This formulation reveals CRR regeneration as a Boltzmann-weighted average over history, with $\Omega$ playing the role of temperature. Low $\Omega$ (low temperature) concentrates the distribution; high $\Omega$ (high temperature) flattens it.

3. Dimensional Analysis

Proper dimensional analysis constrains the form of CRR equations.

Quantity Symbol Dimensions Notes
State $x$ [X] Domain-dependent
Memory density $L$ [C]/[T] Rate of coherence accumulation
Coherence $C$ [C] Accumulated quantity
Threshold $\Omega$ [C] Same as coherence
$C/\Omega$ — dimensionless Required for exponentiation
Historical field $\phi$ [X]/[T] Contributes to $dx/dt$
Remark

The ratio $C/\Omega$ must be dimensionless for the exponential $e^{C/\Omega}$ to be well-defined. This constrains $\Omega$ to have the same dimensions as $C$. In many applications, both are dimensionless (normalised quantities) or carry units of information/entropy.

4. Special Cases

CRR contains simpler dynamical systems as special cases.

Proposition 6 (Markovian limit)

If $L \equiv 0$ (no coherence accumulation) and no ruptures occur, then:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x) + \int_0^t \mathcal{K}(t-\tau) x(\tau) d\tau + \int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) d\tau$$

If additionally $\mathcal{K} \equiv 0$ and $\phi \equiv 0$, this reduces to $\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x)$, an autonomous ODE.

Proposition 7 (Jump-diffusion limit)

If $L \equiv 0$, $\phi \equiv 0$, but ruptures occur at prescribed times $\{t_i\}$ with magnitudes $\{\rho_i\}$ drawn from a distribution, the system becomes a jump process superimposed on the deterministic flow $f$.

Note: These limiting cases establish consistency with existing frameworks but do not constitute derivations of those frameworks from CRR. CRR is proposed as a generalisation that includes additional structure (coherence-triggered ruptures, exponentially-weighted regeneration), not as a foundational theory from which others are derived.

5. The Preservation Parameter

The parameter $p \in [0,1)$ controls post-rupture coherence retention.

Proposition 8 (Effect of p on dynamics)

For constant $L = \ell > 0$:

  • $p = 0$: Complete coherence reset. Inter-rupture interval: $\Delta t = \Omega/\ell$
  • $p \to 1$: Minimal reset. Inter-rupture interval: $\Delta t \to 0$ (singular limit)

The mean inter-rupture interval scales as $\Delta t \propto (1-p)$.

Remark

High $p$ (strong preservation) leads to frequent small ruptures—the system "remembers" most of its coherence and quickly re-accumulates to threshold. Low $p$ (weak preservation) leads to rare large ruptures—more coherence must be rebuilt. This creates qualitatively different dynamical signatures.

The Ω-Symmetry Conjecture

1. Empirical Observation

Analysis of diverse systems exhibiting CRR-like dynamics reveals a striking pattern: the value of $\Omega$ (when appropriately normalised) appears to cluster around specific values that correlate with system symmetry.

Observed pattern: In systems whose dynamics respect a symmetry group $G$, the normalised threshold parameter takes characteristic values:

  • $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (discrete reflection/inversion): $\Omega \approx 1/\pi \approx 0.318$
  • $SO(2)$ symmetry (continuous rotation): $\Omega \approx 1/2\pi \approx 0.159$

2. Geometric Interpretation

We propose a geometric interpretation of these values.

Conjecture 1 (Ω-Phase Relationship)

For systems whose coherence-rupture cycle has underlying phase structure, the threshold parameter satisfies:

$$\Omega = \frac{1}{\varphi}$$

where $\varphi$ is the phase (in radians) traversed during one coherence accumulation cycle.

Remark

For $\mathbb{Z}_2$ systems (half-cycle before rupture): $\varphi = \pi$, giving $\Omega = 1/\pi$.

For $SO(2)$ systems (full-cycle before rupture): $\varphi = 2\pi$, giving $\Omega = 1/2\pi$.

3. The CV Prediction

The conjecture generates a testable prediction about inter-rupture timing variability.

Conjecture 2 (Coefficient of Variation)

For systems with threshold $\Omega$, the coefficient of variation of inter-rupture intervals satisfies:

$$CV = \frac{\sigma_{\Delta t}}{\mu_{\Delta t}} = \frac{\Omega}{2}$$
Derivation sketch

If inter-rupture intervals arise from a threshold-crossing process with variance proportional to $\Omega^2$ (motivated by $\Omega = \sigma^2$ identification below), and mean proportional to $\Omega$, then:

$$CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \propto \frac{\Omega}{\sqrt{\Omega^2}} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\Omega}{2}$$

The factor of 2 arises from the relationship between variance of threshold-crossing times and the underlying variance parameter. A rigorous derivation requires specifying the stochastic model for $L(t)$.

Prediction (Symmetry-specific CV)

Combining Conjectures 1 and 2:

  • $\mathbb{Z}_2$ systems: $CV = \frac{1}{2\pi} \approx 0.159$
  • $SO(2)$ systems: $CV = \frac{1}{4\pi} \approx 0.080$

4. Connection to Variance

Conjecture 3 (Ω-Variance Identification)

The threshold parameter $\Omega$ corresponds to the variance of the system's state distribution:

$$\Omega = \sigma^2$$

where $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(x)$ for appropriately normalised state variable $x$.

Remark

This identification, if correct, connects CRR to Bayesian inference frameworks where precision $\pi = 1/\sigma^2$ plays a central role. Under this identification:

$$\text{Precision} = \pi = \frac{1}{\Omega}$$

For $\mathbb{Z}_2$ systems: Precision $= \pi$ (the number). For $SO(2)$ systems: Precision $= 2\pi$.

5. Summary of Ω Values

Symmetry Phase $\varphi$ $\Omega = 1/\varphi$ CV = $\Omega/2$ Precision = $1/\Omega$
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ $\pi$ $1/\pi \approx 0.318$ $\approx 0.159$ $\pi \approx 3.14$
$SO(2)$ $2\pi$ $1/2\pi \approx 0.159$ $\approx 0.080$ $2\pi \approx 6.28$

6. Epistemic Status

Status of Conjectures 1–3:

These conjectures are motivated by empirical pattern-matching across multiple domains (see Empirical Tests tab). They are not derived from first principles. The geometric interpretation is suggestive but not proven. Alternative explanations for the observed $\Omega$ values may exist.

The value of these conjectures lies in their predictive power: they generate specific numerical predictions (CV values) that can be tested against data from systems not used in formulating the conjectures.

Correspondence with the Free Energy Principle

1. Overview

The Free Energy Principle (FEP) proposes that biological systems maintain their organisation by minimising variational free energy. This section explores mathematical correspondences between CRR and FEP, without claiming that one framework derives from or subsumes the other.

Note: The correspondences described here are structural analogies between mathematical quantities. Whether they reflect deep theoretical unity or merely surface similarity is an open question.

2. Proposed Correspondences

CRR Quantity FEP Analogue Interpretation
Coherence $C$ Accumulated evidence Integrated model fit over time
Memory density $L$ Evidence rate Instantaneous model performance
Threshold $\Omega$ $\sigma^2 = 1/\pi$ (variance/inv. precision) Model uncertainty tolerance
Rupture Model revision Discrete belief update
Regeneration $R$ Belief reconstruction Precision-weighted posterior

3. The Markov Blanket Saturation Hypothesis

In FEP, a Markov blanket is the statistical boundary separating a system from its environment. We propose an interpretation of CRR rupture in these terms.

Conjecture 4 (Markov Blanket Saturation)

A Markov blanket has finite capacity $\Omega$ for mediating information exchange. As coherence $C$ accumulates (representing integrated model-environment coupling), the blanket approaches capacity. When $C = \Omega$:

  1. New observations cannot be assimilated within the current model structure
  2. Prediction errors exceed the model's capacity to minimise them
  3. The system must undergo structural reorganisation (rupture)
Remark

This is an interpretation of the mathematical condition $C \geq \Omega$, not a derivation. The "capacity" metaphor provides intuition but is not a rigorous concept within FEP as currently formulated.

4. Precision and Ω

Under Conjecture 3 ($\Omega = \sigma^2$), the CRR threshold is identified with variance. This connects to FEP through precision:

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{1}{\text{Variance}} = \frac{1}{\Omega}$$
Proposition 9 (Precision-Ω relationship)

If $\Omega = \sigma^2$, then:

  • High $\Omega$ (high variance, low precision): System tolerates uncertainty; ruptures are rare
  • Low $\Omega$ (low variance, high precision): System is tightly constrained; ruptures are frequent
Interpretation

High-precision systems (low $\Omega$) have strict expectations about observations. Small deviations from prediction trigger updates. This manifests as frequent micro-ruptures that reconstitute similar patterns—apparent rigidity.

Low-precision systems (high $\Omega$) have broad expectations. Large accumulated deviations are required before rupture. When rupture occurs, the system can access broader historical states—potential for transformation.

5. Differential Memory Accessibility

The regeneration operator $R(x,t) = \int_0^t \phi(x,\tau) e^{C(x,\tau)/\Omega} d\tau$ creates differential access to history based on $\Omega$.

Proposition 10 (Memory accessibility regimes)

Let $C_{\max} = \max_{\tau \in [0,t]} C(x,\tau)$. The effective historical window contributing to regeneration is:

  • $\Omega \gg C_{\max}$: All history contributes approximately equally
  • $\Omega \ll C_{\max}$: Only states near $\arg\max C$ contribute significantly
  • $\Omega \sim C_{\max}$: Graded contributions across history

The ratio of weights between high-C and low-C states is $e^{(C_{\max} - C_{\min})/\Omega}$. For $\Omega \gg C_{\max}$, this ratio approaches 1. For $\Omega \ll C_{\max}$, the ratio diverges, concentrating weight on the maximum.

6. CRR as Temporal Structure for FEP

Conjecture 5 (CRR-FEP complementarity)

CRR and FEP address complementary aspects of adaptive systems:

  • FEP specifies what states a system tends toward (free energy minima)
  • CRR specifies when transitions occur (coherence reaches threshold) and how reconstruction proceeds (exponentially-weighted history)

A complete theory may require both: FEP provides the variational principle, CRR provides the temporal dynamics.

Epistemic status: The CRR-FEP correspondence is a working hypothesis. Rigorous integration would require: (i) deriving CRR dynamics from FEP principles, or (ii) showing FEP quantities emerge from CRR in appropriate limits, or (iii) demonstrating both arise from a common more fundamental framework. None of these has been accomplished.

Empirical Tests

1. Methodology

The value of CRR lies in its predictive power. This section presents empirical tests against data from systems not used in formulating the framework.

Important distinctions:

  • Post-hoc fitting: Adjusting parameters to match known data. Necessary but not sufficient for validation.
  • Prospective prediction: Using CRR to predict values before examining data. Stronger evidence.

High $R^2$ values indicate good model fit but do not prove correctness. Alternative models may fit equally well.

2. Test Domain: Saltatory Growth

Human growth occurs in discrete bursts (saltations) rather than continuously. This provides a test of CRR's CV predictions.

Data source: Lampl, Veldhuis, & Johnson (1992). High-frequency infant growth measurements.

Observed pattern:

  • 90–95% of observation periods: No measurable growth
  • 5–10% of periods: Discrete growth bursts of 0.5–2.5 cm
CRR Model

Growth is modelled as a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ process (coherence builds in growth plate → threshold → discrete burst → reset).

Prediction: $CV = 1/(2\pi) \approx 0.159$

Result

Observed $CV$ of inter-saltation intervals: $0.15–0.16$

Predicted $CV$: $0.159$

Agreement: Within 5%

3. Test Domain: Wound Healing

Wound healing follows a stereotyped trajectory: inflammation → proliferation → remodelling.

Data source: Standardised wound healing curves (Gurtner et al., 2008).

Observed pattern: Recovery plateaus at approximately 70–80% of original tissue strength.

CRR Model

Healing is a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ regeneration process. The 80% plateau arises because adult tissue operates under $\Omega_{\text{adult}}$, which cannot access developmental coherence patterns (formed under different $\Omega_{\text{developmental}}$).

Result

CRR trajectory fit to standardised healing curves: $R^2 = 0.9989$

Note: This is a post-hoc fit, not a prospective prediction. The high $R^2$ indicates the functional form is appropriate but does not uniquely validate CRR.

4. Test Domain: Muscle Hypertrophy

Muscle growth exhibits "muscle memory"—previously trained muscles regain size faster than naive muscles.

Data source: Staron et al. (1991); Gundersen (2016) on myonuclear retention.

Observed pattern: Retraining time ≈ 30% of original training time.

CRR Model

Myonuclei serve as coherence storage. During detraining, muscle size decreases but myonuclei (and their coherence record) persist. Retraining accesses stored coherence via $e^{C/\Omega}$ weighting.

Prediction: Retraining time ratio ≈ 0.3

Result

10 specific predictions about hypertrophy dynamics tested

Results: 10/10 consistent with data

Model fit: $R^2 = 0.9985$

6. Required Future Tests

To strengthen the empirical case for CRR, the following tests are needed:

  1. Independent replication: CV predictions tested in datasets not used in development
  2. $SO(2)$ systems: Test $CV \approx 0.080$ prediction in circadian/cyclical systems
  3. Cross-domain consistency: Same $\Omega$ values should emerge across systems with same symmetry
  4. Competing models: Compare CRR fit to alternative models (e.g., pure stochastic, neural network)

Current assessment: Preliminary evidence is encouraging but not conclusive. The framework generates specific, testable predictions that distinguish it from generic curve-fitting. Whether CRR captures fundamental dynamics or merely provides a convenient phenomenological description remains to be determined through further empirical work.
